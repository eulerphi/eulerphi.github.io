02: Quality Time

Quality Time is an early childhood literacy app focused on explicitly and systematically teaching the skills of reading (e.g., phonological awareness, decoding, and sight recognition.) The mission is to democratize access to evidence-based instruction and raise the percentage of proficient US 4th grade readers from 33% [1] to an estimated 94% [2][3]. It differs from existing offerings in three key ways.

First, it is designed for and depends on the participation of a caregiver. Although the perception is that caregivers are too busy, there is strong and abundant evidence [4] that caregivers, across cultural, linguistic, and class backgrounds, value their children’s education and want to help. The true problem is they lack the knowledge and skill to effectively and confidently do so [5].

Second, Quality Time can, with a caregiver’s assistance, understand the nuance of a learner’s response and, like an expert tutor, respond with real-time, targeted feedback. For example, when a child blends “side <pause> walk” into “sidewalk,” it is trivial for an adult to classify the response as Repeat, No Response, Too Loud, Too Slow, or Good [7]. This model of guiding and empowering a non-traditional teacher parallels a stunningly successful program started in Kenya (effect size = 1.35) [8] and unlocks a level of adaptiveness and personalization that ensures the success of both child and caregiver.

Third, by guaranteeing success, Quality Time systematically builds the self-concepts of both child and caregiver and strengthens their relationship. The experience of learning to read proves to the child that he or she is “a reader” and can succeed academically. For the caregiver, the experience demonstrates that they have a valuable role to play in their child’s academic development.

Although this project might seem like an impossible moonshot, literacy is the most studied subject in human learning and the research is clear-cut. We know that ~30% of children will learn to read regardless of method, ~50% require explicit, systematic instruction, ~15% require prolonged explicit and systematic instruction, and up to 6% have severe cognitive impairments that make acquiring reading skills extremely difficult [2]. Yet, the status quo knowingly entails that over 60% of US fourth-graders, through no fault of their own, are disproportionately at-risk to drop out of high school [9], to end up incarcerated [10], and to live in poverty [11]. This is inexcusable.

Ideally, the school system would teach every child the skills of reading. Although there are localized success stories, numerous studies indicate that most early childhood teachers lack the deep knowledge necessary to effectively teach reading [12][13]. Worse still, teacher preparation programs are often not teaching the science [14] and even sometimes resistant to it [15]. Hopefully, as the science of reading permeates common knowledge, society will demand that teachers receive the training they require to provide the reading instruction children deserve.

Lastly, although there is no shortage of consumer edtech apps that ostensibly teach reading, they are designed for a child to use alone and employ a touch-centric design. This is a problem because the natural user interface of reading is phonetic [16]. For example, how can an app teach that the grapheme “ee” corresponds to the long e sound without understanding if a child accurately vocalizes it? This limitation necessitates an implicit instructional approach that, as mentioned, only helps the ~30% of students that will learn regardless.
